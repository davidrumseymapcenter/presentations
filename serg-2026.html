SUL as an Allmaps Innovator

Second update: through conversations with Zoe and Stace, we advocated for Stanford to be recognized as an "Innovator" in the Allmaps community. This is a designation that comes with both visibility and direction — and I want to briefly explain why it matters.

Why Allmaps matters for DRMC

  • Open toolkit built on IIIF — no GIS training required to georeference maps
  • Lowers the barrier for researchers, students, and the public
  • Gives us a clear direction for integrating DRMC collections into Earthworks
allmaps.org
Allmaps is a web-based georeferencing toolkit that works with any IIIF-compliant image server — which our collections already are. The big deal is that it largely eliminates the need for GIS training to begin making historical maps machine-readable. Anyone can open a map in a browser and start placing control points. The Innovator designation gives us a recognized place in that ecosystem and an open playing field for connecting more of our collections to Earthworks, Stanford's geospatial discovery platform.

Developing "Core Collections"

  • We lack cohesive, public-facing descriptions of what makes DRMC special
  • What we have tends to revolve around donors, not intellectual themes
  • Hard to communicate our strengths — to researchers, funders, or new colleagues
The maps that make us, us
Third, we're beginning work on what I'm calling "Core Collections" — a way of describing the thematic and intellectual strengths of the DRMC that doesn't just index what donors gave us. Right now, if someone asks what our collection is *about*, the honest answer is that we don't have a clean way to tell that story. Descriptions exist, but they're organized around acquisition history, not around the ideas or geographies or eras the collections illuminate.

Core Collections will help us:

  • Communicate our strengths clearly
  • Showcase collections in discovery and outreach (...including a fall exhibition!)
  • Steward with intention — acquisitions, scanning, conservation, special projects
Core collections descriptions will give us a common vocabulary — for talking to donors, for prioritizing digitization, for building new finding aids and exhibitions, and for orienting new staff. Think of it as the intellectual architecture that sits above individual catalog records. More to come on this as it develops.
