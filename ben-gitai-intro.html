Good afternoon, everyone, and welcome! It’s wonderful to see so many of you here today. I’m Evan Thornberry, the head and curator here at the map center, and before I welcome our speaker, I want to briefly acknowledge the nine year anniversary of the David Rumsey Map Center. This is the first anniversary I’ve had here where I can reflect on a full year.
The map center isn’t as a repository for Stanford’s amazing map collections, or a hub for discussions, research, and events like this. We’re also as an integral part of the student experience. To me, that commitment to students in the last year has been visible in so many ways - whether they were part of the 80 or so classes that visited us or are one of our several fantastic student employees who contribute to making this space a welcoming and open environment for learning and research. Over the last year, we invited new types contributions from students like Umar Patel, who last year developed an app for the Apple Vision Pro, mashing up historical maps and photographs into a virtual workd to explore, or Julie van den Hout and Jenny O’Keefe, who are currently providing invaluable instructional support for the many class visits we see throughout the year.
A couple of weeks ago we partnered with Kim Beil, who is the associate director of Stanford’s ITALIC program, to host the Japanese artist Sohei Nishino. Sohei is known for his city dioramas made up of thousands of photographs taken by the artist over the course of a few months, then compiled into memoryscapes reflecting his lived experience and perceptions of the city.
While at Stanford, Sohei also led a workshop where ITALIC students created their own map of Stanford's campus based on their experiences and memories, using rubbings of objects on campus they pass by everyday, or might be overlooked. Kim describes this as “a map of feeling” or textures of campus, and is the inspiration for this anniversary's keepsake poster, which will always remind us of Sohei's visit, the 2025 ITALIC program, and the roles we can play in the experiences of students here at Stanford.
This a good segway into my introduction to our speaker today, Dr. Ben Gitai. Ben, an architect and researcher at the EPFL in Lausanne Switzerland is the Head Coordinator at EPFL's Habitat Research Center. Ben's work investigates how landscapes evolve – not just physically, but also through history, politics, and the way we perceive them. He’s recently launcehd a fascinating and impactfful project: the Atlas of Landscapes of Paris, a deep dive into the city’s terrain, its stories, and the many visions that have shaped it. Working with Ben these past days has been incredible. His visions are for truly living maps of cities like San Francisco, – incorporating perspectives from residents, wildlife, even cars and bikes we use move through the city. It's not just about boundaries, but understanding how our experiences shape our cultural landscapes.
