I wanted to stert by highlighting our commitment to students beyond being a repository for Stanford's amazing map collections or a hub for discussions, research, and events. As an integral part of the student experience, we strive to make the Map Center a welcoming space for learning and research. In the last academic year, this commitment has been evident in several ways. We've had the pleasure of hosting approximately 80 classes who have visited us, as well as our fantastic student employees who help create a supportive environment for students to learn and grow. Additionally, our student exhibition competition winners, such as Champ, gain valuable experience by working collaboratively with our Assistant Curator Kristina Larsen to design and install an exhibition in the Center. Over the past year, we've also encouraged innovative contributions from students like Umar Patel. Last summer, he developed a unique app for the Apple Vision Pro that combines historical maps and photographs into a virtual world. This unique app allows users to interact with geolocated historical photographs overlaid on a contemporary base map. The app's georeferenced historical map and photographs enables users to explore the past in a highly immersive and engaging way – mashing up collections found here at Stanford Libraries.

Thanks to generous donations, we were able to expand our team by hiring two new student instruction support specialists: Julie van den Hout and Jenny O'Keefe, both graduate students here at Stanford. Over the past academic year, they worked closely with us to provide valuable instructional support for the many classes that visit us throughout the year. One of their notable achievements was developing a primary source set of maps that we will use in teaching here at the Center - focused on maps of California depicted as an island. Although this resource is not yet fully complete, we're pleased with the progress made and look forward to continuing this initiative this year. We're excited to build on this momentum by hiring another Stanford graduate student to join our team in the fall, who will help us advance the next phase of this instruction support specialist role.
Last April, we partnered with Kim Beil, who is the associate director of Stanford’s ITALIC program, to host the Japanese artist Sohei Nishino. Sohei is known for his city dioramas made up of thousands of photographs taken by the artist over the course of a few months, then compiled into memoryscapes reflecting his lived experiences and perceptions of the city.

Sohei Nishino

While at Stanford, Sohei also led a workshop where ITALIC students created their own map of Stanford's campus based on their experiences and memories, using rubbings of objects on campus they pass by everyday, and might be overlooked. Kim describes this as “a map of feeling” or textures of campus, and is the inspiration for this anniversary's keepsake poster, which will always remind us of Sohei's visit, the 2025 ITALIC program, and the roles we can play in the experiences of students here at Stanford.
Now I want to shift and mention a couple of collections related updates. First, this last year we acquired over 60 fire insurance atlases, the bulk of which includes around 50 Sanborn atlases of suburban towns in southern California, a 1913 Baist fire insurance atlas of Los Angeles, and an 1887 Dakin fire insurance atlas of San Francisco.

California Historical Society Acquisition

  • 3000 map sheets
  • 150 rolled maps
  • Several volumes of Sanborns
  • ~1840s - ~1930s
  • Cataloged by Phil Hoehn
The biggest news is probably that Stanford University Libraries acquired the Collections of the California Historical Society – including about 3000 flat maps, 150 rolled maps, and several volumes of fire insturance atlases and block books. These maps represent the state's rich cartographic history, spanning from about the 1840s to the early 20th century. From the copper region of Del Norte County in the north to Date City in Imperial County in the south, these maps offer a glimpse into California's geography, mining, real estate, water supply, road, and railroad history.
